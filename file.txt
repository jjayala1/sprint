        <p class="share-update-card__update-text">Is still valid the 9-5 schedule?
 
Every day more people become knowledge workers or entrepreneurs or freelancers and they can be remotely worldwide. 
 
Yes I know, if I have a medical emergency, I don't want to die because the doctor have flexible schedule üíâ.
 
But with knowledge workers, I can't get it. 
 
This is my top 3 myth vs reality list: 
 
MYTH 1: 
If you need something from a coworker, it's better if everybody is at the same time 
 
REALITY: 
Most of the time, you don't need anything immediately, and again I'm talking about knowledge workers, not about the fireman üë®üöí.
 
MYTH 2: 
If you're on time, you show loyalty and commitment to your work and your boss and your boss' boss... 
 
REALITY: 
Long hours result in lots and lots of time lost, the light conversations in the water dispenser, in the printer, or voting for who will bring the coffees. 
 
MYTH 3: 
If you arrive on time, you can go out on time. 
 
REALITY: 
Maybe if you are the type of person that counts the minutes to run and punch your card, it's ok. But when you are so immersed in a project, the time expands and contracts in inexplicable ways. If you don't get lost most of the time, maybe you're not in the right place. 
 
CONCLUSION 
 
I still have to stick to my schedule, and I love my job, but I see a different future.¬†A better one. 
 
Maybe the future has come and a lot of people haven't realized it yet.

        <p class="share-update-card__update-text">How to solve any problem?

In my 21 years career, I've faced a lot of problems to solve.

After researching for problem-solving frameworks I've adapted some ideas and developed my framework.

I developed this framework while solving issues in <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/signup/cold-join?session_redirect=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Elinkedin%2Ecom%2Ffeed%2Fhashtag%2Fsoftware&amp;trk=public_post_share-update_update-text" data-tracking-control-name="public_post_share-update_update-text" data-tracking-will-navigate rel="nofollow">#software</a>, but I realized it's extensible enough to any problem.

1 Identify the problem
If it's your problem you know what it is, but if you have to solve other people's problems, look at the problem as they see it.

2 Look for the obvious cause, asking Why questions

3 Look for the next cause

4 Repeat until you find the smallest cause

5 Figure out how to solve the deepest cause

6 Repeat until you solve the obvious cause, it's common that the obvious cause gets solved automatically when you solve deeper causes.

There are some good frameworks like Fishbone, Scatter plot, 5 Whys, but until you adapt and make yours the framework all of that is just theory. Don't reinvent the wheel, but make the process yours.

Tomorrow I'll give you an example using this framework to solve a trivial problem.

Do you use a framework to solve problems or do you use a more empirical approach?

        <p class="share-update-card__update-text">Are you a good boss/leader for your team?

Yesterday I talked about how to improve the relationship with your boss.

And the key is effective communication, as I said yesterday, it should be in 2 ways, so today I give my top 5 tips to improve the relationship with your team.

1. Understand that everyone in your team is different, you have to treat all of them with respect but know them. It's your job to get the best of each one.

2. Be clear with inputs. What supplies do they need to do the job? what information do they need? what tools, formats, templates, data, etc?

3. Be clear with the outputs. What do you exactly need? be as specific as you can, again which format, colors, size, etc depending on the output.

4. Make sure your team is clear about points 2 and 3. Repeat until it's crystal clear, believe me, time used in planning it's not a waste. it's worst having a result that does not serve you.

5. You're the boss, which means that you have to serve them, remove the problems that avoid them being productive.

Bonus tip
6. Golden rule. Treat your team how you would like to be treated.

        <p class="share-update-card__update-text">Why having a nasty boss could be your fault?

Effective communication is the pillar in every relationship. Communication is in both ways, otherwise is a monologue.

If you can't stand your boss, maybe you aren't communicating at all.

Your boss asks,  gives you projects, activities, etc. but it should be clear to both parts what's the expected result.

It could be obvious, but it isn't. You can apply quickly a few changes to improve the relationship.

These are my 5 tips to improve the communication with your boss

1. Ask, ask and ask until the activity is crystal clear to both, formats, colors, tools to employ, etc.

2. Take notes, it's annoying for a boss to repeat the same again and again and again...

3. If you have enough expertise, make suggestions, identify flaws. Always in private, otherwise you make your boss look bad and it's the last thing you want.

4. If it's a recurrent activity try to automate it, at least partially, you'll increase your productivity and you'll have a happy boss. My rule of thumb is to automate if I have to do it more than 3 times.

5. If you did the first point well, check the result before sending it to your boss, if the task needs to be blue send it in blue not in violet (I'm colorblind,  sometimes happens üò¨)

Even with all those points, you can have an impossible boss, at least try for some weeks.

        <p class="share-update-card__update-text">Voltaire said, "The best is the enemy of the good."

Perfectionism is most of the time a bad thing because maintains us in constant dissatisfaction, and always looking for a defect to improve.

But it's a 2-edged weapon if we use perfectionism as an excuse to do mediocre work.

As a software developer, I can't write the perfect script, the perfect web page, or having the perfect performance. But I can learn from each piece of code I write and improve the next one. If a developer continues coding like months or years ago, it means he/she hasn't grown and is stuck in the past.

The same occurs in each part of our lives, we love stability, continuity, but that leads us to repeat the same story again and again.

At the end of my life, I don't want to live 80 times the same year I want to live 80 different years.

We just need to give just the next step towards an awesome life.
